Portal Recreation Demo

View on Artstation   |   Github Repository

Portal was a fantastic game and followed by the masterpiece that is portal 2, and I wanted to look a little deeper into how it was made. The portal itself has multiple approaches involving camera effects, room duplicates, or raycasts, and each has their own ups and downs. I decided on camera effects, because they were the easiest to implement in an intuitive way. Each portal has its own second camera that renders the scene and passes the ‘image texture’ to the other portal. The effect worked amazingly, after a good amount of polish, and I would love to dive into the other methods at some point too.

Tank Boss Battle

View on Artstation   |   Github Repository

A boss battle! With tanks! What could be better? I've long been a fan of an old and simple game called Wii Play Tanks, and wanted to implement some of those ideas into a game. The perfect opportunity came as a university project, where we had to build a simple tank controller and a boss that could move and attack. I expanded on this idea and built a simple intro cutscene to build up the boss and gave him multiple stages to make it more progressive. On a more technical level, I implemented the observer pattern for more efficient game references, the state pattern for cleaner AI, and object pooling (for both sound effects and the bullets) for better performance.

Coding & Tools

Dynamic Sound System

View on Artstation   |   Github Respository

A music and sound effect manager, packed with object pooling for better performance, customizable audio clip references, and a WIP music manager.

Sound Effects:

There are three ways to use a Sound Effect Reference with this tool, either directly input the audio clip, create a Sound Data Object (Allows you to adjust volume, pitch, etc. of a clip), or a Sound Event (Can reference multiple clips and select a random volume and pitch from specified ranges). The Sound Events allow for unique and interesting sfx during gameplay that vary each time it is played. For simplicity, each of these just has to be referenced by a script and can say _soundObject.Play();

Music Manager:

The music manager I am building has layers of complexity and at its core allow for dynamic soundtracks with toggleable levels (Ex: Normal and Combat) that can be mixed together. It can fade tracks in and out and allows for different music tracks to be played in different areas of the level.

The Influence of Earthbound (1995)

Earthbound, known as Mother II in Japan, redefined the very popular genre of role-playing games (RPGs) with their unique, down to earth, story approach. Development of the game was a joint effort between Ape Inc and HAL Laboratories, led by Shigesato Itoi. The developers’ intent with this RPG was to get the player invested in a story using emotional storytelling and characters with strong and quirky personalities. More specifically, Itoi “wanted to create a game (EarthBound) with real characters; characters whom players would recognize in the people around them” (Mato). Earthbound achieves these effects on the player through unconventional level design, hidden dialog boxes, and personal narrative.

The level design in Earthbound is unconventional and diverse. The world is large with various styles for each area while still having minute details. The developers focused on worldbuilding more than anything else. This broadens the experience of the game and is more enjoyable across different levels. Another key factor is the lack of a hub world. This way, the level feels more like an open world, where the player can explore or continue the story as they like. Through all the world design and artistic choices, Itoi’s goal is to make the player feel emotions, from happiness and satisfaction to loneliness.

At the core of the game, there is the ever-useful text box for dialogue. This common popup is manipulated and emphasized purposefully for the context of the moment, giving clearer intent. On top of this, clever writing builds and strengthens an emotional connection with the player. Around the time Earthbound was released, it was common to see text boxes that display information or simple dialog, but Earthbound goes beyond that with quirky effects and hidden dialog across the world that makes those who find it feel special. This is an interesting choice, as most RPGs are more streamlined and appeal solely to fantasy, while Earthbound is surreal and feels like an entirely different genre. Beyond this, there is such an incredible touch of personality in the writing, where even simple stuff like saving, battle dialogue, and tips can be memorable, ridiculous, or off-putting.

The designers kept the narrative directed and concise, but let it appear open to the player. This choice allowed most of the game to be about all the characters and the towns, rather than the main story, The narrative has a quirky and personal appeal, leading the player to become more emotionally invested into the story. This was intentional, as Itoi would present odd ideas to his team and eventually “people would start chiming in with other similar ideas of their own. Those links of reckless wildness are what the Mother games are built on” (Schreier). Such a creative open dialogue within the developmental team made Earthbound into a unique and intriguing game that became so much more than just a cut and dry RPG. This creative “wildness” is directly applied to the narrative and to environmental features.

Earthbound is a unique game that has touched many hearts and inspired many other games. It “remains an underappreciated video game franchise with a dedicated cult following, but with it, some of the most popular indie games today would not exist” (Jensen). One game that was directly influenced by Earthbound is Undertale (2015), by following the unique genre set by Earthbound. The game is about the dialogue and world building more than the gameplay itself. These kinds of games are an intimate experience for the player, allowing for them to invest in the immersive world presented by the non-linear level design and quirky characters.
